Don’t be ‘At yourself' and take a rest

August 8th 2014

It feels right, now that some time has elapsed, to reflect a little on the Eight Week Course that I ran here last spring. The spring really was glorious. The sun shone, birds sang and the mountains accompanied us as we explored and experimented with this thing called Mindfulness. I felt so fortunate to be offering this training in such an environment. The presence of the solid stable mountains sure helped us settle and begin to learn 'the art of just being’.

And as I ask myself now what impressions have really stayed with me the following occurs to me…

The theme of how we are always ‘picking' at ourselves, trying to ‘fix' ourselves or 'solve the problem' of ourselves and our ‘flawed’ lives. Is that familiar? There is great value in becoming aware of just how ‘wearing’ this is, of what this costs us emotionally and physically.

And Mindfulness offers an alternative way of approaching life; inviting us to 'let ourselves be', to develop a practice of being present to and with ourselves moment by moment by moment, without any attempt to fix or change.

This isn’t easy though. That habit of ‘picking’ at ourselves, judging, fixing is fairly engrained but with commitment and time something starts to shift. What a relief! We need this - this pausing, just being, in a little stillness and feeling a comforting quiet inside. Moments when we get to feel whole and connected and settled. Moments of letting our agendas drop and just being in the unfolding stream of our lives. This is such good medicine for minds and bodies. This simple practice is a deep salve for frayed minds, hearts and bodies.

The other theme that emerged was just how uncomfortable it often is, in our bodies and in our minds, when we begin to practice mindfulness. Minds and bodies squirm, restlessness, agitation even, arises within us. It takes courage and a lot of perseverance. Did I mention that it takes A LOT of perseverance? :) And of course then the judging mind goes “I am no good at this either”. This is, be reassured, all entirely natural and with space, time and a lot of kindness towards ourselves we settle into these practices. Ah….. but in the beginning that restless mind and body can really be a pain! :)

And finally another theme that came through strongly was how we have lost the art of resting Our bodies and minds are over-stimulated and over-taxed and we seem to have forgotten the art of resting. And I don’t mean zoning out e.g in front of the telly. I do mean the deliberate, intentional act of regularly taking time to slow right down and do the gentle activities that feel nurturing and restorative. We live in such a culture of striving it is perhaps not so very surprising that rest has been a casualty.

As the spring eight week course drew to a close though and participants explored their plans for carrying forward their mindful living there was a real desire to build in routines e.g at bedtime, that would allow rest to re-establish its place at the centre of our lives.

Remarking on the Unremarkable

April 14th 2014

One map that underpins Mindfulness can be described in the following way;

All experience has a 'feeling tone’, either Pleasant, Unpleasant or neutral.

Our natural tendency is to chase the pleasant, avoid or push away the negative and ‘zone out’ the neutral.

Mindfulness as a practice is the opening equally to all three - to just be wholeheartedly present to and aware of, a ALL experience - regardless. Bringing kindly awareness to it all.

In particular the experience which registers with us as neutral is of interest to me at the moment. This has been a very active practice for me for these last few weeks. I have been actively seeking out the neutral -bringing it into the foreground, as it were.

The following might offer some flavour;

...the cuff of a wellington boot rubs along a calf muscle , the sound and texture of granola scraped off a breakfast bowl, the faces of drivers in oncoming cars, the sound of my own footsteps, yes often the sound of my own footsteps, many many sounds, the feel of the door handle under my hand, this is what makes up a life, isn’t it - the unremarkable yet when noticed…..

One of my motivations in this practice is that i believe that at the end of our lives all these moments would be suddenly revealed as ‘the heart of life’ ; the insight that these ‘neutral’ moments are really what life was made of - and that perhaps you missed it, that perhaps you hadn’t shown up for your own life.

Jon Kabat-Zinn the founder of The Mindfulness Based Stress Reduction programme that I teach, has an often quoted phrase

“you only have moments to live” ….the sound of a door opening, the feel of the pen between thumb and forefinger, the welcome silence that rushes in at that moment when the kitchen fan is turned off…..

Mind-weather 2

April 2nd 2014

I am going to speak about mind weather again, because that language is around for me at the moment.

It is important I think to become aware that the weather of the mind is not “who” we are- but is rather something that ‘arises’ within us. Arises and falls away actually because everything is constantly moving and changing if you watch carefully enough.

Mind states come and go. Mind weather. Low moods, restless moods, gladness, agitated thoughts, busyness, it rolls on, moving through us.

In mindfulness practice we learn to find a still place and a kind place, within ourselves, that just observes this with quiet understanding. And we really come to directly experience that we are not our mind states. Some storms coming through us are fierce and some days it is a gentle breeze. But always the sky is there, simply a backdrop to it all. Just like our mindful awareness. The cognitive therapists call this metacognitive awareness - the ability to observe our thoughts and feelings coming and going and it is a very important and protective resource; especially if we are prone to acutely low moods or intense mind states.

May we all learn to find a place where we are not pulled off balance (or where we can return quickly enough to balance) by each passing change in the weather.

Mind-weather

March 14th 2014

Today I am feeling somewhat rattled and a bit churned up inside. Yesterday I was full of energy and today, mostly because I have been hit by a virus, I feel very different.

So reflecting on this i ask “ How can mindfulness as a philosophy and a practice support me in this?" It is a familiar one, isn’t it, this rapid change of mood or energy. (it is kind of, like the funny way we are permanently surprised by the changes in weather in this part of the world :))

Certainly Mindfulness helps me notice the changes of 'mind-state’ as opposed to being overtaken by them. it has taught me a great deal about how mood states permanently change. There isn’t much wisdom in resisting this process is there? it’s called being a human being.

Also these practices have taught me not to try to hold on to how I was feeling, even moments ago; how to just keep re-booting, starting afresh as it were. It is a lot less exhausting living this way, not setting myself up in opposition to the flow of life; and somehow the changing weather of the mind isn’t quite as troublesome as it once was.
